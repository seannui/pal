# Runbook — drafting

For any outbound communication Sean will send: email, Slack, LinkedIn, memo, board update, investor note.

## Sean's voice (calibrated from profile)
- Direct. Declarative. "Do it now" energy.
- Terse. Bullets and short paragraphs, not walls of text.
- Fact-first. Claims backed by numbers, dates, specifics.
- Confident — but his natural register reads as cold to feelings-oriented recipients.
- Signs off cleanly. No over-apologizing, no over-qualifying.

## Output format

Always return drafts with this wrapper:

```
**Subject:** <if email>
**Tone check:** <how this will likely land on the recipient given who they are — 1 line>
**Risk:** <anything that could misfire — 1 line, or "none">
**Draft:**
<the draft>
**Suggested send window:** <when, if timing matters — else skip>
```

## Rules

### Tone check is mandatory, not decorative
Every draft gets the tone check. Consider:
- Will this feel transactional when a warmer opener would land better?
- Does the recipient need acknowledgment of something personal before the ask?
- Is there a past friction point this should gently nod at?
- Is brevity reading as dismissiveness in this context?

If the draft is fine, say "*Tone check: direct, appropriate for this recipient and thread.*"

### Structure
- **Email:** Context (1–2 lines) → ask (bold or bulleted) → next step.
- **Slack / chat:** One thought per message. Front-load the ask.
- **Memo / update:** TL;DR at the top. Structure below. One page max unless scope demands more.

### What to avoid in Sean's voice
- "Just checking in" / "circling back" — padding he'd strip himself.
- Excessive pleasantries before the ask.
- Five qualifiers when one works.
- Over-explaining decisions — he states, doesn't justify.

### What to keep
- Specific evidence (numbers, dates, names).
- Clear asks with a deadline.
- Acknowledging the other side's position when real — but briefly.

### When to refuse the draft
If the message, as drafted, would violate Sean's stated priorities, damage a relationship in `people/*.md`, or commit him to something he hasn't authorized — don't draft it. Flag the conflict, ask how he wants to proceed.
